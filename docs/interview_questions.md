# Interview Questions & Model Answers

Prepared responses for common Business Intelligence Engineering interview questions,
anchored to this project. Use these as talking points, not scripts.

---

## Architecture & Design

**Q: Walk me through this project's architecture.**

> "The pipeline follows a classic data lake pattern on AWS. Raw synthetic data
> is generated by Python scripts and cleaned into Snappy-compressed Parquet files
> partitioned by year and month on S3. AWS Glue Data Catalog stores the schema
> definitions and partition metadata, making the data queryable via Amazon Athena
> without any servers to manage. Eleven SQL files cover DDL, validation, KPIs,
> and advanced analytics including window functions and RFM segmentation. QuickSight
> pulls from Athena via SPICE — an in-memory cache — to serve three dashboards
> with sub-second load times. The whole thing is reproducible from a single `git clone`."

---

**Q: Why did you choose Athena over Redshift?**

> "Athena is the right choice when you need serverless, ad-hoc SQL over a data lake
> and your query volume is irregular. For this project — a portfolio with no sustained
> concurrent users — Athena's pay-per-TB-scanned model keeps costs near zero between
> runs. Redshift makes more sense when you have many concurrent dashboard users who
> need consistently low latency, or when you're running complex joins across dozens of
> large tables. The architectural trade-off is: Athena = operational simplicity and
> cost-efficiency for irregular workloads; Redshift = performance and concurrency
> for sustained BI workloads."

---

**Q: How does partitioning improve query performance here?**

> "Partitioning by year and month means Athena's query planner can use partition
> pruning to skip entire directory paths in S3 when a query includes a date filter.
> A query for 'all 2023 Q4 transactions' only reads 3 month directories instead of
> all 48 in the 4-year dataset — roughly a 94% reduction in data scanned. Since
> Athena charges per TB scanned, this directly cuts cost. In testing, a full-dataset
> scan was ~800 MB; with a current-year filter and partitioning, the same query
> scanned ~200 MB."

## SQL & Analytics

**Q: Explain your RFM segmentation approach.**

> "RFM stands for Recency, Frequency, and Monetary — three dimensions that capture
> customer purchasing behavior. I computed Recency as days since a customer's last
> order relative to the most recent order in the dataset. Frequency is a count of
> distinct transactions. Monetary is total net revenue per customer. I scored each
> dimension 1–5 using `NTILE(5)` window functions in Athena, then applied business
> rules to group customers into named segments — Champions, Loyal Customers, At-Risk,
> Lost, and so on. This goes beyond a simple decile ranking; it produces actionable
> segments a CRM team can immediately act on. See `08_customer_segmentation.sql`."

---

**Q: What window functions did you use and why?**

> "Several — each for a specific analytical purpose:
> - `SUM() OVER (ORDER BY date ROWS BETWEEN UNBOUNDED PRECEDING AND CURRENT ROW)` for running totals (YTD revenue)
> - `LAG()` for month-over-month growth rate comparison
> - `RANK()` and `DENSE_RANK()` for product and customer revenue ranking
> - `NTILE(5)` for RFM quintile scoring
> - `PERCENT_RANK()` to express a customer's relative position in the LTV distribution
> - `FIRST_VALUE()` / `LAST_VALUE()` for first and last purchase date per customer
> Window functions let me do all of this in a single pass over the data without self-joins."

---

**Q: How do you handle data quality?**

> "Three-layer approach. First, Python cleaning (`clean_sales_data.py`) enforces schema,
> removes duplicates, caps outliers, and validates referential integrity before any data
> reaches S3. Second, `validate_dataset.py` runs 15+ named checks — completeness, uniqueness,
> domain validity, date ordering, statistical plausibility — and exits with a non-zero code on
> any failure, acting as a pipeline gate. Third, `11_data_quality_checks.sql` runs a
> DQ scorecard in Athena after each load, producing a PASS/FAIL row per check that can
> be surfaced as a monitoring dashboard. The goal is to fail loudly and early rather than
> serve corrupt data silently."

## Business Impact & Stakeholder Communication

**Q: How would you explain the cohort retention heatmap to a non-technical stakeholder?**

> "I'd say: 'Each row is a group of customers who signed up in the same month.
> Each column shows what percentage of that group came back and made a purchase
> in a later month. A dark cell means lots of them came back; a light cell means
> most didn't. Reading left to right on any row tells you how loyal that customer
> group turned out to be over time. If you see rows getting lighter quickly, we're
> losing customers fast after their first purchase — that's a sign we need a better
> onboarding or follow-up strategy.'"

---

**Q: A VP sees two different revenue numbers — one from your dashboard, one from Finance's Excel sheet. How do you handle that?**

> "First, I don't assume my number is right. I ask Finance to walk me through their
> calculation: what transactions are included, what date field they're using, whether
> they're including returns or taxes, and what fiscal calendar they follow. Then I trace
> my SQL back to the raw data using the same filters. Usually the discrepancy comes from
> one of four things: different date fields (order_date vs. ship_date), different revenue
> definitions (gross vs. net), different treatment of discounts, or a partition mismatch
> (Finance includes Dec 31 but the dashboard partition didn't pick it up yet). The fix
> is always: align definitions in writing, update `kpi_definitions.md`, and republish. Never
> paper over the gap — the next time the numbers diverge, you'll be in the same meeting."

## Cost & Operations

**Q: How would you reduce Athena costs in production?**

> "Four levers: (1) Parquet + Snappy — already applied; reduces scan volume ~70% vs CSV.
> (2) Hive partitioning — applied; reduces scan by 85–95% for date-filtered queries.
> (3) CTAS materialization — pre-aggregate frequently-run KPI queries into Athena tables;
> dashboards read the aggregated table instead of scanning raw transactions.
> (4) Workgroup data usage limits — I set a 10 GB per-query cutoff and a $10/month
> budget alarm on the workgroup to catch runaway queries before they become expensive.
> In this portfolio workload, monthly Athena cost is under $1."

---

**Q: How would you productionize this pipeline?**

> "Three main upgrades: (1) Replace numbered SQL files with dbt models — gives dependency
> management, incremental runs, and built-in testing. (2) Orchestrate with AWS Step Functions
> or Apache Airflow — makes each stage explicit, retryable, and observable.
> (3) Add Terraform for IaC so the entire AWS stack can be destroyed and recreated in one
> command across dev/staging/prod environments. I'd also add Delta Lake or Iceberg for
> ACID compliance on the S3 layer — enabling late-arriving data corrections without full
> partition rewrites."
